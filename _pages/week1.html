    • Wednesday, May 15th - Science Communication (#1/28 of class):

    Learning Objectives:
    1. LO1: Begin to build a classroom culture
    2. LO2: Define scientific knowledge: How do we know what we know & how does that affect science communication?
    3. LO3: Describe principles of good science communication
    4. LO4: Identify brain regions that we will commonly come across in class

    Agenda:

    1. Instructor introduction (~3 min): Get to know Christina as a scholar and person (LO1).
    2. Icebreaker (~5 min): Get to know each other as scholars and people (LO1).
    3. Discuss the syllabus, get to know you survey, and classroom norms, so that you know how to be successful in this class (~5 min; LO1).
    4. Gallery walk: handouts on what constitutes scientific knowledge, retractions, etc. (~20 min; LO2).
    5. Handouts with different perspectives on Science Comunnication: read & generate in class list of principles we'll follow (~10 min; LO3).
    6. Apply list of science communication principles we generated to a SciComm article (~10 min; LO3).
    7. Use 3D Brain, a free app (or online version here), to find regions on worksheet (~20 min; LO4).
    8. Reflections on class and any concerns/questions you, students, have (e.g., minute paper, ~5 min; LO1).

    Readings:

    • None - we will read some articles in class!

    Thursday, May 16th - Cognitive Methods (#2/28 of class):

    Learning Objectives:
    1. LO1: Continue to build a classroom culture & discuss science communication.
    2. LO2: Describe the advantages and disadvantages, as well as basic mechanisms of, each methodology
    3. LO3: Summarize the types of questions typically answered with each methodology
    4. LO4: Assess current problems within psychological methodology such as WEIRD samples and the lack of open science

    Agenda:

    1. Icebreaker (~5 min): Get to know each other as scholars and people (LO1).
    2. Warm-up Gallery walk: Look at tweets, determine audience, how images supported claim, etc. (handout, ~10 min; LO1).
    3. Study vignettes on correlational, experimental methods, and cognitive methods (~30 min; LO2, LO3).
    4. Jeopardy style Check for Understanding in relation to methodology questions (~10 min; LO2, LO3).
    5. Summarize current barriers and issues within the open science framework (~10 min; LO4).
    6. Explore the concept of p-hacking (also here) as a class (LO4).
    7. Reflections on class and any concerns/questions you, students, have (e.g., minute paper, ~5 min).

    Friday, May 17th - Perception (#3/28 of class):

    Learning Objectives:
    1. LO1: Continue to build a classroom culture & discuss science communication.
    2. LO2: Describe the basic principles thought to underlie perception and perceptual illusions.
    3. LO3: Deconstruct and analyze the findings of an academic journal article.
    4. LO4: Compare and contrast models of science communication.

    Agenda:

    1. Demo (on blind spot & more) & explanation of basic perceptual illusions and principles (LO1, LO2).
    2. Break off into pairs and apply the reading worksheet to Gruters et al. (2018) (LO1, LO2).
    3. Come together as a class to discuss the main takeaways of the Gruters papers & perception generally (LO3).
    4. Generate main takeaways from Ed Yong's coverage of the Gruters et al. (2018) article; what is missing when compared to our reading worksheets? (LO3, LO4)
    5. Compare Dean (2018) to Yong (2018) - how do they differ? Do these pieces fit our list of good SciComm? How might you develop your own voice? (LO4)
    6. If time permits, critique the Mozart Effect or Bem's ESP effect to continue discussion of scientific knowledge & encourage critical thinking (LO3).
    7. Reflections on class and any concerns/questions you, students, have (e.g., minute paper).
     Readings

    Article – skim:
    • Gruters, K. G., Murphy, D. L. K., Jenson, C. D., Smith, D. W., Shera, C. A., & Groh, J. M. (2018). The eardrums move when the eyes move: A multisensory effect on the mechanics of hearing. Proceedings of the National Academy of Sciences, 115(6), E1309–E1318. https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.1717948115. PDF here
    News coverage:
    • Yong, E. (2018, January 23). When Your Eyes Move, So Do Your Eardrums. Retrieved from The Atlantic website: https://www.theatlantic.com/science/archive/2018/01/when-your-eyes-move-so-do-your-eardrums/551237/. PDF here
